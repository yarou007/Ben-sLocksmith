Filing an Insurance Claim for Commercial Glass Damage: Step-by-Step

Most commercial property insurance covers glass breakage — but the claim process trips up a lot of business owners. Here's exactly how to do it right.

Step 1: Document Everything Before Cleanup

Before any glass is swept up, removed, or boarded, photograph the damage extensively. Take photos from multiple angles, distances, and perspectives. Photograph any evidence of how the break occurred — impact marks, forced entry signs, weather damage. These photos are the foundation of your insurance claim. If it's safe to do so, don't move anything before photographing.

Step 2: Call Your Glass Company and Insurer at the Same Time

Call your commercial glass company — 703-244-0559 — for immediate response. At the same time (or while waiting for the tech), call your commercial property insurer's claims line to open a claim. The claim date is the day you notify them — the sooner, the better. Give them the address, time of incident, and a brief description of the damage.

Step 3: Get Professional Documentation from Your Glazier

A professional commercial glass company will provide, as standard: a written damage assessment with measurements, photographs of the damage and affected areas, a professional estimate in the format required by commercial insurance adjusters, and notation of the glass type and framing system for accurate replacement cost calculation.

This documentation is what the adjuster uses to approve your claim. Adjusters move faster when the documentation is complete and professional — this is one area where using a specialized commercial glass company pays for itself immediately.

Step 4: What's Covered and What's Not

Standard commercial property insurance typically covers: vandalism and malicious mischief (break-ins, spray paint, deliberate damage), theft-related glass breakage, wind and storm damage, vehicle impact, and in some policies, thermal breakage (spontaneous tempered glass failure).

Typically not covered: glass breakage due to normal wear, gradual deterioration, or faulty original installation. Most glass riders also have a per-pane deductible — check yours.
