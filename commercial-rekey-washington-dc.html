Start with the problem

Rekey the access plan, not automatically every piece of hardware

Commercial rekeying changes which keys operate existing cylinders when the hardware remains suitable. The project can involve one suite, several departments or a managed group of openings.

A clear scope starts with who should have access, which doors belong together and whether any existing master key relationships must change. That planning prevents accidental access between tenants or departments.

Diagnosis and scope

Employee, vendor and lost-key changes

When a key is not returned or its copies cannot be accounted for, rekeying can restore control without changing otherwise functional locks. The affected access group should be identified before cylinders are changed.

Key points

  • Employee separation and role changes
  • Vendor or contractor access removal
  • Lost or unreturned commercial keys
  • Unknown key-copy concerns

Commercial decision support

Tenant turnover and multi-suite rekeys

Tenant transitions may affect suite entries, shared rooms and management access differently. Each opening should be assigned to the correct tenant or common-area group.

Key points

  • Office and retail tenant turnover
  • Suite-by-suite cylinder inventory
  • Shared utility and management areas
  • Phased work for occupied properties

Service details

Master-key and key-control planning

A master-key change should reflect real operational roles rather than simply copying the old hierarchy. Property, facility and department access can be separated into understandable levels.

Key points

  • Access groups by team, floor or suite
  • Management and emergency-access planning
  • Key-issue and return responsibilities
  • Future turnover and expansion considerations

Commercial FAQ

Questions about this commercial service

When should a business rekey its locks?

Common reasons include employee changes, tenant turnover, lost keys, unreturned vendor keys and an access plan that no longer matches current roles.

Is rekeying different from replacing a commercial lock?

Yes. Rekeying changes which key operates a serviceable cylinder. Replacement changes hardware and may be appropriate when the lock or cylinder is damaged or unsuitable.

Can several suites keep separate keys while management retains access?

A planned key hierarchy can separate tenant access from approved management access. The exact arrangement depends on the existing cylinders and the authorized access plan.

How should a multi-door commercial rekey be organized?

List each affected opening, current key group and intended access role. A door-by-door schedule helps distinguish keyed-alike groups, separate access and any hardware that needs repair before rekeying.
