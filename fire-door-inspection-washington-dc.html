Start with the problem

Scope the inspection before anyone arrives

A fire door inspection request is scheduled work, so the best first step is a clear opening count and building contact—not an emergency-only call path. Property managers, facility teams, building engineers, schools, medical facilities and contractors can use the request form to describe the property, the number of openings and any deadline or authority request.

NFPA 80 addresses inspection, testing and maintenance of fire door assemblies, but the standard, adopted code and authority having jurisdiction can affect what a specific building needs. This page is service-planning information, not a legal determination. Share the governing request or project documents when available.

Diagnosis and scope

Information to prepare

A useful quote starts with the size and purpose of the inspection. Exact counts are helpful but not required for the first request; an approximate number of doors or openings is enough to begin a scope conversation.

Key points

  • Building type, occupied hours and access contact
  • Approximate number of fire-rated openings and floors
  • Known labels, prior reports or deficiency lists
  • Deadline, requesting party and desired reporting format
  • Whether corrective work should be quoted separately
  • Site rules, escort needs and scheduling constraints

Commercial decision support

Inspection and repair are different scopes

An inspection records observed conditions against the agreed scope. Repair changes an opening or its hardware. Keeping those steps distinct makes documentation clearer and lets the property decide how corrections will be authorized.

If a door does not close, latch or secure now, identify that condition in the form. A separate repair visit may be more appropriate than waiting for a broader inspection. Written reports and deficiency-correction services must be confirmed in the estimate because those business capabilities are not assumed.

Key points

  • Requested inspection scope: observe, operate and document agreed items
  • Correction planning: prioritize and price observed deficiencies
  • Repair: adjust, repair or replace authorized components
  • Reinspection: verify corrected items when included in scope

Service details

What an opening review may cover

The exact checklist belongs in the approved scope. Common review categories include labels, visible damage, clearances, glazing, hinges, latching, closing action and unauthorized field modifications. A site-specific requirement may add or remove items.

Key points

  • Door, frame and label condition
  • Self-closing and positive-latching operation
  • Hinges, fasteners and coordinators where present
  • Gaps, clearances and obstructions
  • Panic or fire-exit hardware condition
  • Visible alterations that require further review

Commercial FAQ

Questions about this commercial service

Who usually requests a commercial fire door inspection?

Requests may come from an owner, property or facility manager, building engineer, contractor, insurer, consultant or authority. Share the actual request so the service scope matches it.

Should I request inspection or repair?

Choose inspection when you need an opening review or documented findings. Choose repair when a known door, closer, latch or exit device needs corrective work. The scopes can be quoted separately.

Are written reports available?

Report availability, format, photos and follow-up requirements must be confirmed in the written service scope before scheduling. Use the form to describe what the requesting party needs.

What does NFPA 80 mean for my Washington DC property?

NFPA 80 is a consensus standard concerning fire doors and other opening protectives. Applicable code, adopted edition and enforcement are project-specific; confirm requirements with the authority having jurisdiction or your design/code professional.
